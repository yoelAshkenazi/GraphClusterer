3D printing is a rapidly evolving technology with diverse applications across multiple fields, including medicine, engineering, and manufacturing. It is an additive manufacturing process that allows for the creation of complex 3D objects with intricate shapes and geometries. The versatility of 3D printing lies in its ability to use various materials, such as polymers, nanocomposites, and biomaterials, each with unique properties that cater to specific application requirements. 3D printing is making significant strides in the medical field, particularly in tissue engineering and biomedical applications, where researchers are exploring the use of 3D printing to create scaffolds for tissue regeneration, biosensors, and biomedical detection devices. Nanomaterials play a crucial role in enhancing the properties of 3D-printed medical products. In the engineering and manufacturing sectors, 3D printing is being utilized to create complex structures and functional devices, offering advantages such as high resolution, high accuracy, and unique customizability. 3D printing also enables the prototyping of parts with high complexity and precision in the water treatment industry and is finding applications in optics and photonics. However, challenges and limitations are associated with 3D printing, such as the need for suitable materials and printing resolution, build volume, and speed constraints, which researchers are working to overcome by exploring different techniques, materials, and hybrid approaches.
