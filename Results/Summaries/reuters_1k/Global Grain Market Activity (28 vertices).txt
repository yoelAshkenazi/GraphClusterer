Argentina has reported increased exports of bread wheat, maize, and sorghum. The U.S. Department of Agriculture is actively involved in the market, accepting bids for export bonuses to countries like Sri Lanka and Algeria. Canada has also reported substantial exports of wheat, barley, rapeseed, and flaxseed for the 1986/87 season. Soviet operators are fixing timecharters for grain imports, and dry cargo futures have shown strong gains due to higher rates for grain business from the U.S. Gulf to Japan. Cuba's sugar harvest is facing setbacks due to unseasonable rains, which have affected harvesting and milling operations, especially in the central and western parts of the island, leading to a production shortfall of 800,000 tonnes. Japan has also slightly reduced its sugar consumption estimate, revising it down to 623,300 tonnes on a refined basis for the April-June period. The U.S. Department of Agriculture has been making adjustments to credit guarantees, switching wheat credit guarantees for Jordan under the Export Credit Guarantee Program to the Intermediate Export Credit Guarantee Program, and has offered a new durum wheat initiative to Algeria, allowing U.S. exporters to sell an additional 300,000 tonnes of durum wheat at competitive prices in the Algerian market. There are rumors of a potential wheat bonus offer to the Soviet Union, with speculation that the Reagan administration may make an export enhancement offer soon. The U.S. Department of Agriculture has been issuing generic certificates to provide free corn and wheat supplies, aiming to make U.S. grain more competitive globally.
